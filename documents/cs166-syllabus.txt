CS166: Modeling and Analysis of Complex Systems
Spring 2026

Course Description
Have you ever wondered how people use computers to make decisions? In this course, you will learn
how to apply advanced modeling techniques such as cellular automata, network concepts from graph
theory, probability theory, and Monte Carlo simulation to analyze and predict the behavior of social,
physical, and economic systems. You will learn from specific examples of portfolio management, traffic
flow management, and analyzing social networks.
We start the course by diving straight into your first simulation in the first week. You write structured code
in Python from the first until the last week of the course. After the first week, we cover three modeling
frameworks—cellular automata for modeling interactions on grids of cells, networks for more general
interactions between nodes in a graph, and Monte Carlo simulations showing how we can use simulation
to generate random numbers and how we can use random numbers to drive simulations of complex
phenomena. We cover each framework's theoretical (mathematical) and practical (implementation)
aspects.
Please read the course prerequisites and course policies to be adequately prepared for the start of the
course.

Prerequisite knowledge & Summer prep
Programming in Python
We use Python 3 in this course and avoid using new functionalities provided by more recent versions
than Python 3.6 unnecessarily. The recommended Python 3 distribution is Anaconda, which you can
download for free from https://www.anaconda.com/products/individual. Make sure the Anaconda
distribution is installed and working on your computer.
You should know how to write functions, for-loops, while-loops, conditional (if-elif-else) statements.


You should be comfortable with manipulating basic data structures like classes, lists, dictionaries,
and strings.
You should have a passing familiarity with NumPy and SciPy.
You should be comfortable plotting data using Matplotlib.
Resources
Work through Part 1 (“Getting started with Python for science”) of SciPy Lecture Notes for a
practical introduction to basic programming in Python for computational statistics.
Linear algebra
straightforward products of vectors and matrices,
some experience in computing eigenvectors and eigenvalues will be beneficial but is not required.
Probability and statistics
the sum and product rules,
marginal and conditional probabilities,
Bayes’ rule,
probabilities over discrete and continuous variables,
probability density and cumulative density functions,
expected values, mean, variance, and standard deviation.
Resources
The Statistics Fundamentals YouTube channel by Josh Starmer (StatQuest). Easy-to-understand
explanations with clear visualizations.
Statistics and Probability by Khan Academy. A good resource for students who feel they lack
foundational probability knowledge or are relatively new to probability. There are lots of exercises
available.
Exercises on statistics from Oxford College.

Professionalism
This course has a #professionalism LO. Writing high-quality reports with easily interpretable plots,
precise mathematics, and well-documented code is essential to using statistical modeling. You should
use the feedback on this LO to improve your work presentation and how you conduct yourself throughout
the course. This LO is targeted in every assignment.
Pre-class work
The typical class format of CS166 places a strong emphasis on pre-class work. The pre-class work
almost always forms the basis of an in-class activity, and you will not learn meaningfully from the class
session if you have not thoroughly worked through the pre-class exercises.
You do not have to get everything right, but you have to try and show your work. If you get stuck
and find that you cannot complete the pre-class work, explain why. Please explain what you
attempted and why you think it didn't work. Many students have documented their learning process
and were able to get "unstuck" by doing so.
Teaching assistants will provide formative feedback on your pre-class work to help you learn from
your mistakes and improve.
Not completing pre-class work to a satisfactory level will result in penalties on #ClassParticipation.
You will occasionally get graded on pre-class work as part of your in-class assessment alongside
poll and video grading. Which sessions get graded on pre-class work is determined randomly but
still following pedagogical directives.
Makeup work
1. Do all the assigned reading and pre-class work and watch the video recording of the class.
2. Meet with one of the TAs for ~15 minutes to discuss the class session and address any questions
you still have.
Make-up work should be completed within two weeks. E.g., if you miss a Tuesday class, complete the
make-up work before class on the Tuesday two weeks later.
If you do not complete the make-up work within four class sessions, you will receive a "bonus" 0 on
#responsibility for the session.
Spring break and Quinquatria do not count towards the make-up work clock.
Any make-up work not complete by the end of Week 15 will be receive a 0 on #responsibility.
