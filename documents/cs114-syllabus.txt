Course Description
Recommended: Read the 2-page summary of this syllabus
We apply the theory of probability and statistics to make sense of real-world observations in scientific,
social, economic, medical, and various other contexts. In this course (and, in more detail, in the
concentration courses of the Statistics & Data Science track), we learn how to answer the types of
questions used to find structure in messy real-world data.
What is the value of a variable we can not observe or measure directly? How do we use the data
we can observe to gain information about the variables we cannot observe? (This is called
parameter estimation.)
How well do different or competing models explain our data or observations? Which model is the
best at explaining the available data? (This is called model selection.)
What are the plausible or most likely values of variables we can potentially observe but haven’t
observed yet? (This is called prediction.)
What would happen if we intervened in a system? What would have happened if we had not
intervened in a system? (This is called causal inference.)
In CS114, we focus on the theory underlying these questions and on applications related to parameter
estimation, prediction, and model selection. (For more on causal inference, amongst other topics, see
CS130.)

Preparing for the course
This course builds on Formal Analyses, where you covered concepts and applications of Probability and
Statistics but without going into much mathematical detail. In CS114, we focus on the mathematical and
computational aspects of Probability and Statistics, looking at the fundamentals and the applications of
these fields.
To prepare for CS114, you can do the following:
Review Formal Analyses. For basic probability and statistics, we assume and expand on the
conceptual understanding you developed in FA.
Review CS111. For continuous random variables and distributions, we assume knowledge of
Calculus and elementary mathematical functions like the exponential and logarithm functions.
Install Python. The recommended Python 3 distribution is Anaconda, which you can download for
free. You may also use Google Colab, Deepnote, or other online tools for viewing or creating
Python notebooks.
Review Python basics. We won't write complicated Python code in CS114 but we do use Python
generally and in the NumPy, SciPy, and SymPy packages in particular to simulate random
processes and to analyze data. For a practical introduction to basic programming in Python for


computational statistics, work through Sections 1.1 and 1.2 from “Getting started with Python for
science” of the SciPy Lecture Notes.

Unit 3: Continuous and multivariate distributions
For continuous random variables, we cannot determine the probabilities of particular outcomes but we
can for ranges or sets of outcomes. We discover how we can use Calculus to go from discrete random
variables to continuous random variables by applying limit theorems, turning counts into sizes of
intervals and sets, and sums into integrals.

Participation in CS114
You will likely be graded for participation in almost all classes. Participation in CS114 is set up to
encourage and reward engagement with the class discussion, both to support your learning of CS114
content and to practice being engaged in team discussions. Your participation score is based on both the
frequency of the comments and your preparedness for the class.
You are responsible for being an engaged participant. In short, it is your responsibility to be an
engaged member of the discussion, rather than the instructor’s responsibility to force reluctant
participation. There will be some “cold calling" in CS114, but much less compared to many other
courses at Minerva, and if you only speak when cold called you might receive low participation
scores. With that said, there may be a rare day where you don’t end up saying anything, and that is
fine; there is no need to inform your instructor about anything (e.g., “I was feeling sick that day…”);
across the semester, you should make sure that on most days you are an active member of the
class discussion.
**There is no particular metric to “hack” for a good participation score. **A good participation score
in CS114 is not about talking a certain number of times (e.g., twice in a session) or minutes (e.g.,
2-3 minutes in a session) or anything else. Instead, it is about being an engaged member of the
class discussion in general. Notably, “holistic” participation scores such as this are a common part
of many classrooms around the world, and we are excited about how the video recording of our
Forum classes allows us to review the actual participation rather than an instructor relying on their
memory.
Pre-class work policy
Regardless of whether you attend the class or not, you will be graded on your pre-class works. PCWs
come with extensive guides but since the PCWs are mainly about new topics and some might be
challenging, you will be mainly graded on your effort. Please make sure you put in significant amount of
work into the PCWs. You cannot use AI to help you with the pre-class work and we'll do an extensive
check on your submissions. If you are absent in a session, you will receive a 0 on the PCW until you
submit them as a pdf to the instructor within exactly 7 days of the class. Then your submitted work will be
graded and the new grade will replace the 0.
