Course Description
Predictive and Causal Inference for Decision Making
How do we use data to predict the future, understand the past, and estimate the impacts of events, policies, and decisions? This
course explores the application of predictive and causal statistical inference for decision-making. This course is designed to
challenge and enrich students at all levels of coding and quantitative ability--everyone from seasoned computer science
students to students with no statistics or coding background beyond CS50/51.
The first part of the course focuses on useful, practical predictive modeling methods, reviewing foundational methods like linear
regression and introducing non-parametric techniques such as random forests and nonlinear local regression. The second part
(the main part) shifts to causal inference, equipping you with the toolkit necessary to evaluate impact in real-world settings.
This course provides more than academic credit; it provides a professional edge. Students who have taken this class have
leveraged skills learned here to win highly competitive positions in public policy, business, and the sciences.

Unit 2: Statistical Learning (Classification and Prediction)
We begin the statistical learning (classification and prediction) unit by reviewing linear regression. We then describe logistic
regression (logit) as an extension suitable for binary classification problems. We also discuss out-of-sample prediction and
cross-validation to assess and address overfitting. The unit ends with an introduction to random forest modeling.

Overview
There is no make-up work in CS130, but you must always submit pre-class work, even if you are going to be absent.
Pre-class work
The typical class format of CS130 places a strong emphasis on pre-class work. The pre-class work almost always forms the
basis of an activity in class, and you will not learn meaningfully from the class session if you have not worked through the pre-
class exercises in detail.
You have to do the pre-class work every time.
Even if you intend to skip a class, you have to do the pre-class work, and you will still get graded on it.
You do not have to get everything right, but you have to try, and you have to show your work. If you get stuck and find that
you are unable to complete the pre-class work, explain why. Explain what you attempted and why you think it didn’t work.
Teaching assistants will provide formative feedback on your pre-class work to help you learn from your mistakes and
improve.
You will frequently get graded on pre-class work as part of your in-class assessment.
Which sessions get graded on pre-class work is determined randomly.
Not completing pre-class work to a satisfactory level will result in a low score.
Not engaging meaningfully with the pre-class work at all will result in a score of 0.
Make-up work
There is no make-up work, but remember that you have to submit your pre-class work even if you will be absent from
class.
It is in your best interest to watch the class recording when it is available. If the video recording is unavailable for any
reason (for example, due to a technical issue), please contact your instructor. Where possible, a recording of another
section of the same course will be shared with you.
When the going gets tough...


This class is designed to be challenging, and not just because of its coding and statistics; beyond these technical challenges,
the class is conceptually challenging as well. Therefore, I anticipate that from time to time, every student will get stuck and have
questions. When this happens to you, do not fret, try not to get discouraged, and do not struggle on your own for more than 30-
45 minutes without seeking assistance from peers/TAs/professors.
Using AI tools
Everyone has to follow Minerva's AI policies. If you use an AI tools to complete your submission, and/or you collaborated with
another student, please explain at the top of your submission.
Transparency
Relevant code or a link to code must be included in all assignment submissions. One excellent and free way to share code is to
use GitHub gists. Info on that here (same procedure for sharing code in any programming language, not just Python):
